Addressing Syracuse’s Vacant Property Crisis: Inside the Greater Syracuse Land Bank’s Efforts

The Fight Against Vacant Properties in Syracuse, New York

By: Camila Santiago

A home in Syracuse, New York, hits the market for $2,000. Credit: The New York Post/Realtor.com

Housing in Syracuse has become an increasingly critical issue, with vacant properties emerging as a significant obstacle to affordable housing for both families and college students.

As a Syracuse University student, I’ve personally struggled with finding an apartment that fits my budget, an experience shared by many in the city. This challenge, coupled with the broader housing crisis, prompted me to explore the issue of vacant properties and how it affects the availability of residential housing.

In examining the data on vacant properties, I focused on residential spaces to better understand their distribution and ownership. My analysis revealed that a substantial number of these properties were owned by the Greater Syracuse Land Bank (GSPDC), an organization established in 2012 to address Syracuse’s vacant property problem.

To delve deeper into their role and strategies, I reached out to Katelyn Wright, the Executive Director of the Land Bank, and conducted an interview to understand their efforts to revitalize neighborhoods and improve housing options.

The Greater Syracuse Land Bank’s mission is centered on transforming vacant and abandoned properties into productive assets. This process involves either renovating homes that can be saved or demolishing structures beyond repair to create opportunities for new development. They currently have 642 demolitions completed.

Since its founding, the organization has made notable progress in reducing the number of vacant properties in Syracuse.

“There were significantly more vacant buildings when the Land Bank first started back in 2012,” Wright explained. “We’ve reduced that number by 44%.” This substantial decrease underscores the impact of the Land Bank’s efforts and its contribution to addressing the city’s housing challenges.

One of the primary ways the Land Bank acquires properties is through Syracuse’s tax foreclosure process. However, this method comes with inherent challenges. “We get all of the city’s tax foreclosures, so we don’t have a whole lot of say in what we get or when,” Wright shared.

Because the organization does not control which properties it receives or their condition, the Land Bank often faces the challenge of working with buildings that require substantial repairs or demolition.

Illegal dumping on Land Bank properties further complicates their efforts. Wright noted that illegal dumping is a persistent and costly problem. “There is an extraordinary amount of trash dumped on our properties each year,” she said, estimating that the Land Bank spends more than $250,000 annually on cleaning up debris. This financial burden reduces the resources available for renovating or redeveloping properties.

Additionally, the age and condition of many vacant properties present significant obstacles. Wright pointed out that most of the buildings were constructed before 1970 and often contain black lead-based paint.

This material poses serious health risks, particularly for children, and requires careful management during renovations or demolitions. Addressing these environmental hazards adds layers of complexity to the Land Bank’s work, further emphasizing the challenges of their mission.

Despite these obstacles, the Land Bank has made meaningful strides in revitalizing Syracuse neighborhoods. By collaborating with developers, community organizations, and city officials, the Land Bank works to return properties to productive use.

These efforts help reduce urban blight, create housing opportunities, and foster neighborhood renewal. The organization’s work is particularly valuable in addressing Syracuse’s housing shortage, which has left many residents struggling to find affordable and suitable homes.

The challenges facing the Land Bank are reflective of broader issues tied to Syracuse’s economic struggles and population decline. These factors have contributed to a surplus of abandoned and vacant properties, making it even more difficult to maintain vibrant, livable neighborhoods.

Addressing these problems requires a multi-faceted approach, including increased funding for redevelopment, policy reforms to discourage illegal dumping, and incentives to attract developers and homeowners to reinvest in Syracuse’s housing market.

Through its work, the Greater Syracuse Land Bank has become a cornerstone of Syracuse’s efforts to tackle its housing crisis. Their progress is evident after their 44% reduction in vacant properties, mentioned by Katelyn Wright, but the challenges they face reflect the complexity of addressing urban blight and housing shortages.

Looking ahead, the Land Bank’s continued success will depend on community involvement, innovative partnerships, and sustainable funding. Their ability to overcome obstacles like illegal dumping, aging infrastructure, and limited control over property acquisition will play a crucial role in shaping Syracuse’s future. For residents, students, and families alike, the Land Bank’s mission offers hope for a city with revitalized neighborhoods and improved access to affordable housing.

By transforming neglected properties into assets that benefit the community, the Greater Syracuse Land Bank is helping to build a stronger and more connected Syracuse.

As their work progresses, they remain an essential part of the solution to the city’s ongoing housing challenges, bringing life back to neighborhoods and creating opportunities for growth and renewal. This also serves as a model for other cities, fostering pride and stability in Syracuse’s neighborhoods while contributing to long-term growth and revitalization across the community.
